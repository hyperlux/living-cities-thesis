## üèõÔ∏è GOVERNANCE & INSTITUTIONAL THEORY (Samo Burja)

### "The City Makes the Civilization" (Palladium, Summer 2024)

Key arguments:
1. **Cities as coordination landscapes manifested physically**
   - "Where you live is always intimately tied to where you stand in society"
   - Physical address = social rank

2. **Technology made cities MORE important, not less**
   - COVID remote work experiment failed to dislodge urban power
   - "It wasn't cities that took a hit, but commutes"
   - Internet amplified urban cultural footprints (parasocial participation)

3. **New cities as political transformation engines**
   - "Creating a new capital city was, for most of history, one of the best ways to transform society"
   - New Washington D.C. would escape existing institutional predation

> "There is no way to reimagine or reorganize society without reimagining and reorganizing cities."

**Thesis implication:** Living cities thesis needs a theory of *city founding* not just city improvement. The incumbent institutional landscape prevents reform.

### "The End of Industrial Society" (Palladium, 2021)

Still highly relevant framework:

1. **Post-industrial ‚â† next stage of progress**
   - It's exhaustion, not evolution
   - "A society after and without industrial civilization"

2. **White-collar overproduction as pacification**
   - Real downward mobility disguised as paper upward mobility
   - Knowledge economy as "control system for containing militant potential of demobilized worker units"

3. **Factory traditionalism (Germany/Japan)**
   - Industrial preservation through social democracy
   - Flash-frozen revolution preventing new firm formation

**Thesis gap:** Where does distributed manufacturing fit? If industrial society is exhausted, what's the production model for living cities? Artisanal urbanism? Automated micro-factories? The thesis needs an explicit economic base.

### Great Founder Theory

Core insight: Functional institutions are the exception, not the norm. They require exceptional "live players" to create and maintain.

- Most institutions are run by "dead players" executing inherited playbooks
- Reform requires rising elites with new social technology
- Knowledge succession is the critical failure mode

**Application to urban futures:** Charter cities need founders, not just frameworks. The Pr√≥spera lawsuit shows what happens when the founder leaves and the "dead player" government wakes up.

## ‚ùì GAPS OUR THESIS MIGHT BE MISSING

### 1. The Legitimacy Problem
Network states assume you can opt-out of existing states. But states don't let high-value citizens leave easily. Tax, military service, property claims. What's the realistic path from "startup society" to "recognized sovereignty"?

### 2. The Reproduction Problem
All these communities are demographic sinks‚Äîyoung professionals without children. Who raises the next generation? Living cities need a family formation theory.

### 3. The Class Problem
$1,500/month in Malaysia is still filtering for global 5%. What's the model for non-elite participation? Service workers? Legacy citizens of repurposed infrastructure?

### 4. The Production Problem (Critical)
What do living cities *make*? If post-industrial society is exhausted, and China has industrial production, what's the economic base? Knowledge work + AI is insufficient‚Äîneed physical production story.

### 5. The Violence Problem
BBC piece on Praxis: "Strikers got into frequent standoffs and scuffles... labor militia eventually integrated into state systems of violence." Who provides security in network states? What prevents capture by whoever has the most guns?

### 6. The Founder Succession Problem
Per Great Founder Theory: What happens when Balaji dies? When the charismatic leader is gone, what prevents institutional decay?
