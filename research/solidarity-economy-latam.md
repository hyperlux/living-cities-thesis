# Solidarity Economy in Latin America: A Deep Dive (2025)

## Executive Summary

Latin America has been the global epicenter of solidarity economy experimentation since the 1990s. From Argentina's barter networks during economic collapse to Bolivia's constitutional enshrining of *Vivir Bien*, the region offers critical lessons for alternative economic organization. This document surveys key initiatives, their scale, policy frameworks, and documented outcomes.

---

## 1. Argentina: Barter Networks (Redes de Trueque)

### Historical Context

Argentina's barter networks emerged as a crisis response during the devastating 1998-2002 economic depression, when:
- GDP contracted by **28%** (1998-2002)
- Over **50%** of the population fell below the poverty line
- **25%** were classified as indigent (basic needs unmet)
- **Seven out of ten children** lived in poverty at the crisis peak (2002)
- Unemployment reached **20%+**

### The Crédito System

**Origin:** The Crédito emerged in 1995 as a complementary currency to facilitate barter exchanges.

**Scale at Peak (2001-2002):**
- Estimated **2.5-6 million participants** at peak
- Over **5,000 barter clubs (nodos)** operating nationwide
- Transactions reached equivalent of **hundreds of millions of USD annually**
- Some networks issued demurrage currency (losing value over time to encourage circulation)

**Structure:**
- Local barter clubs (*nodos*) organized exchanges
- Regional networks (*Red Global de Trueque*, *Red de Trueque Solidario*)
- Crédito notes issued as internal exchange medium
- Democratic governance at club level

**Provincial Quasi-Currencies:**
During the crisis, provincial governments also issued complementary currencies:
- Buenos Aires's **Patacón** (strongest provincial currency)
- National **LECOP** (government-issued quasi-currency)
- These circulated alongside the Crédito network currencies

### Outcomes and Lessons

**Positive:**
- Provided survival mechanism for millions during acute crisis
- Demonstrated capacity for community self-organization
- Preserved social fabric during economic collapse
- Created alternative economic circuits outside formal banking

**Challenges:**
- Hyperinflation of Crédito (counterfeiting, oversupply)
- Collapse alongside economic recovery (2003-2005)
- Quality control issues with goods/services
- Political capture by some factions

**Legacy (2025):**
- Networks largely dormant but institutional memory preserved
- Periodically reactivated during subsequent crises
- Influenced global complementary currency movements
- Model studied worldwide for crisis response planning

## 2. Brazil: Solidarity Economy Policy Framework

### Institutional Architecture

**SENAES (Secretaria Nacional de Economia Solidária):**
- Established **2003** under President Lula da Silva
- Located within Ministry of Labor and Employment
- First national-level solidarity economy secretariat in the world
- **Abolished in 2019** under Bolsonaro administration
- **Reconstituted 2023** under Lula's third term

**Policy Framework:**
- National Policy on Solidarity Economy (2012)
- National System of Fair and Solidarity Trade
- Solidarity Economy public procurement preferences
- Community development banks authorization

### Scale and Data

**National Solidarity Economy Mapping (SIES):**
Latest comprehensive data (2013 mapping):
- **19,708** solidarity economy enterprises (EES) registered
- **1.4 million** workers in solidarity economy
- **44.9%** located in Northeast region
- **60.7%** in rural areas
- **54.8%** involved in collective production

**Sectoral Distribution:**
- Agriculture and extractivism: 35%
- Commerce and services: 27%
- Artisan production: 23%
- Recycling cooperatives: 8%
- Other: 7%

**Community Development Banks:**
- **Over 100** community development banks operating
- **Palmas Bank** (Fortaleza, 1998) pioneering model
- Issue local social currencies (moedas sociais)
- Provide microcredit without traditional collateral

### Solidarity Economy in the Lula 3.0 Era (2023-Present)

**Recent Developments:**
- Reconstitution of SENAES (now under Ministry of Development)
- New National Solidarity Economy Plan in development
- Focus on integration with food security programs
- Emphasis on platform cooperativism and digital inclusion
- Articulation with agroecology and family farming policies

**Estimated Current Scale (2024-2025):**
- **~25,000** solidarity economy enterprises (preliminary)
- **1.5+ million** associated workers
- Growing focus on urban periphery initiatives
- Integration with social assistance programs (Bolsa Família linkages)

### Key Outcomes

**Documented Impacts:**
- Income generation for marginalized populations
- Democratic workplace governance
- Environmental sustainability practices
- Women's economic empowerment (majority of participants in some sectors)
- Food sovereignty contributions

**Challenges:**
- Access to credit and markets
- Technical assistance capacity
- Regulatory framework limitations
- Competition with conventional economy
- Political vulnerability (2019-2022 dismantling)

## 3. Ecuador: Buen Vivir / Sumak Kawsay

### Constitutional Framework

**2008 Constitution** - First in the world to enshrine:
- **Rights of Nature** (Articles 71-74)
- **Buen Vivir** as guiding principle
- **Food Sovereignty** (Article 281)
- **Solidarity Economy** recognition
- Prohibition of GMO seeds/crops (Article 15)

### Theoretical Foundation

**Sumak Kawsay** (Quechua):
- Sumak = plenitude, sublime, excellent, beautiful
- Kawsay = life, being
- Translation: "life in plenitude" or "splendid existence"
- Indigenous Amazonian Kichwa systematization (1994, OPIP)

**Five Pillars:**
1. No life without knowledge/wisdom (*Tucu Yachay*)
2. Connection to Mother Earth (*Pacha Mama*)
3. Life is healthy (*Hambi Kawsay*)
4. Life is collective (*Sumak Kamaña*)
5. We all have dreams/aspirations (*Hatun Muskuy*)

**Core Principles:**
- Relationality (interconnection of all elements)
- Reciprocity (relationship between worlds, humans, nature)
- Complementarity and cooperation over accumulation
- Harmony with community, family, Nature, universe

### Policy Implementation

**Under Correa Administration (2007-2017):**
- National Development Plan framed around Buen Vivir
- SENPLADES (national planning secretariat) integration
- Social spending increases
- Yasuni-ITT Initiative (attempted, later abandoned)
- Poverty reduction: **25%** living in poverty (2019)

**Economic Indicators:**
- GDP: **$130.53 billion** (nominal, 2025)
- GDP per capita: **$7,210** (nominal, 2025)
- Human Development Index: **0.777** (high, 88th globally)
- Gini coefficient: **45.4** (medium inequality)

**Solidarity Economy Integration:**
- Constitutional recognition of social and solidarity economy
- Support for community-based enterprises
- Indigenous community governance recognition
- Plurinational state structure

### 2023 Yasuni Referendum

**Landmark Decision:**
- National referendum led to cessation of oil drilling in Block 43
- Located within Yasuni National Park (UNESCO Biosphere Reserve)
- Home to Indigenous Tagaeri and Taromenane peoples
- Victory for Rights of Nature constitutional provisions
- **First time a country votes to leave oil in the ground**

### Critical Assessment

**Achievements:**
- Constitutional paradigm shift
- International influence on Rights of Nature
- Poverty and inequality reduction
- Indigenous rights recognition

**Contradictions:**
- Continued extractivism dependence (oil = 32% exports)
- Mining expansion in practice
- Indigenous community conflicts
- Gap between constitutional vision and implementation

## 4. Bolivia: Vivir Bien / Suma Qamaña

### Constitutional Foundation

**2009 Constitution:**
- Plurinational State of Bolivia
- Suma Qamaña (Aymara for "living well") as governing principle
- **Law of the Rights of Mother Earth** (2010)
- Framework Law of Mother Earth and Integral Development (2012)
- Community economy recognition

### Evo Morales Era (2006-2019)

**Economic Outcomes:**
- GDP quadrupled: **$9.57 billion → $42.4 billion**
- GDP per capita doubled
- Extreme poverty: **38% → 18%**
- Gini coefficient: **0.60 → 0.446** (significant reduction)
- Lowest accumulated inflation in Latin America (by 2021)

**Key Policies:**
- Nationalization of natural gas and mining
- Social redistribution programs
- Indigenous governance recognition
- Community economy support
- Universal social programs (Bono Juancito Pinto, Renta Dignidad)

**Current Status (2025):**
- GDP: **$56.34 billion** (nominal)
- GDP per capita: **$4,530** (nominal)
- HDI: **0.733** (high)
- Population below poverty line: **36.5%** (2023)
- New economic crisis emerging (late 2024)

### Vivir Bien Framework

**Philosophical Base:**
- Rejection of infinite growth paradigm
- Harmony between humans and nature
- Community over individual
- Complementarity over competition
- Decolonization of development discourse

**Practical Implementation:**
- Community governance recognition
- Indigenous territory autonomies
- Plural economy framework (state, community, private, social-cooperative)
- Environmental protection (in principle)

### Critical Assessment

**Achievements:**
- Dramatic poverty reduction
- Indigenous political empowerment
- Economic stability (until recently)
- Alternative development discourse

**Contradictions:**
- Continued extractivism dependence
- Conflicts with Indigenous communities over development projects
- TIPNIS road controversy
- Gap between discourse and environmental practice
- Current economic crisis (2024-2025)

## 5. Venezuela: Communes and Communal Economy

### Bolivarian Missions and Social Programs

**Scale (Peak Period 2003-2013):**
- Over **30 social missions** established
- Funded by oil revenues (PDVSA)
- Focus on health, education, food, housing

**Key Missions:**
- **Misión Robinson** (literacy) - millions reached
- **Misión Barrio Adentro** (healthcare) - primary/secondary coverage
- **Misión Mercal** (subsidized food distribution)
- **Misión Hábitat/Gran Misión Vivienda** (housing)

**Documented Outcomes (Chávez era):**
- Poverty: **49.4% → 30.2%** (1999-2006)
- Extreme poverty: **21.7% → 9.9%** (same period)
- Infant mortality: declined 5.9% (1999-2013)
- Gini coefficient: **47.8 → 44.8** (1999-2006)
- Social spending: **44.6%** of 2007 budget

### Communal Council System

**Legal Framework:**
- Law of Communal Councils (2006, amended 2009)
- Law of Communes (2010)
- Organic Law of Popular Power (2010)

**Structure:**
- Communal Councils: neighborhood-level (~200-400 families urban, ~50 rural)
- Communes: aggregation of communal councils
- Communal Parliament (Poder Comunal)

**Estimated Scale (Various Years):**
- **~45,000** communal councils registered (2015 estimate)
- **~1,500** communes in formation (various estimates)
- Direct funding mechanisms (bypassing traditional municipalities)

### Current Status (2025)

**Economic Collapse Context:**
- Hyperinflation, GDP collapse, mass emigration
- Missions severely degraded
- **~80%** of Barrio Adentro establishments abandoned (2014 reports)
- Resource constraints limiting communal economy

**Communal Economy Persistence:**
- Some communes continue operating
- Urban agriculture initiatives
- Community-managed enterprises
- Survival economy functions

**Critical Assessment:**
- Mixed legacy: real social gains followed by collapse
- Dependence on oil revenues proved fatal
- Political capture of social programs
- Sustainability without external resource flows unclear
- Some grassroots communal organization persists

## 6. Mexico: Cooperative Sector and Ejidos

### Ejido System

**Historical Background:**
- Post-revolutionary land reform (1917 Constitution, Article 27)
- Major distribution under Cárdenas (1934-1940)
- Communal land with usufruct rights, state ownership
- **Neoliberal reform 1992**: privatization allowed

**Current Scale:**
- **~32,000** ejidos and agrarian communities
- **~5.6 million** ejidatarios
- **~103 million hectares** (~52% of national territory)
- Continued existence despite 1992 reforms

### Cooperative Sector

**Legal Framework:**
- General Law of Cooperative Societies (1994, amended)
- Constitutional recognition (Article 25)

**Scale (Estimates):**
- **~15,000** registered cooperatives
- **~8 million** members
- Key sectors: agriculture, fishing, savings/credit, consumption

**Prominent Examples:**
- **UCIRI** (Unión de Comunidades Indígenas de la Región del Istmo) - fair trade coffee pioneer
- **Tosepan Titataniske** - 35,000+ indigenous families
- Fishing cooperatives (Pacific coast)
- Credit unions (cajas de ahorro)

### Social and Solidarity Economy Movement

**Recent Developments:**
- State-level solidarity economy laws (emerging)
- Social economy enterprises growing
- Platform cooperativism interest
- Links to agroecology movement

**Current Context (AMLO/Sheinbaum Era):**
- Sembrando Vida program (rural employment)
- Social program expansion
- Limited formal solidarity economy policy
- Ejido system maintained

## 8. Synthesis: Lessons and Patterns

### Common Themes Across Cases

1. **Crisis as Catalyst:** Solidarity economy initiatives often emerge or expand during economic/political crises (Argentina 2001, Venezuela 2000s)

2. **Constitutional Anchoring:** Ecuador and Bolivia demonstrate power of constitutional recognition for alternative paradigms

3. **Scale Matters:** Brazil shows importance of national-level institutional support (and vulnerability when removed)

4. **Extractivism Contradiction:** Even progressive governments maintaining resource extraction dependence

5. **Community Governance:** Indigenous governance models provide alternatives to both state and market

### Quantitative Summary

| Country | Key Metric | Peak/Latest |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Argentina | Barter network participants | ~6 million (2002) |
| Brazil | Solidarity economy enterprises | ~25,000 (2024 est.) |
| Brazil | Workers in solidarity economy | ~1.5 million |
| Ecuador | Constitutional Rights of Nature | Yes (2008) |
| Bolivia | Poverty reduction (2006-2019) | 38% → 18% extreme |
| Venezuela | Communal councils | ~45,000 registered |
| Mexico | Ejido system coverage | ~103 million hectares |

### Critical Gaps in Data

- **Real-time tracking** of solidarity economy enterprises lacking
- **Outcome measurement** beyond economic indicators limited
- **Comparative analysis** of different models needed
- **Longitudinal studies** of sustainability rare
- **Integration metrics** with formal economy unclear

## 9. Implications for Living Cities Thesis

### Urban-Rural Connections

- Solidarity economy bridges urban consumption and rural production
- Community agriculture and agroecology links
- Migration remittances in solidarity circuits
- Food sovereignty as urban-rural nexus

### Governance Models

- Communal councils offer neighborhood-scale precedent
- Participatory budgeting connections
- Multi-scalar democratic governance
- Indigenous governance wisdom

### Economic Transformation

- Post-growth frameworks (Buen Vivir)
- Plural economies (Bolivia model)
- Complementary currencies for local resilience
- Cooperative enterprise as default form

### Challenges for Urban Application

- Density and anonymity vs. community trust
- Scale of urban resource flows
- Integration with existing infrastructure
- Political economy of urban land
