# The Political Economy of Living Cities

## Power, Conflict, and the Struggle to Transform Urban Space

The transition to living cities is not merely a technical challenge of better design or more sophisticated ecological engineering. It is fundamentally a political project that redistributes power, wealth, and life chances across urban populations. Every decision about green infrastructure—where it goes, who pays for it, who benefits from it—reflects and reinforces particular configurations of political and economic power. Understanding the political economy of living cities requires grappling with winners and losers, coalition building and opposition, and the fraught question of whether meaningful urban transformation can emerge incrementally or demands more radical ruptures with existing arrangements.

This chapter examines the political terrain that any serious effort at ecological urbanism must navigate. The analysis proceeds from a recognition that cities are not neutral containers but contested spaces where different groups advance competing claims on urban futures. The living city vision, however appealing in the abstract, threatens entrenched interests while creating opportunities for new coalitions. Success depends not just on demonstrating technical feasibility or ecological necessity but on building the political power necessary to overcome resistance and institutionalize new urban forms.

## Winners and Losers: The Distributional Politics of Green Urbanism

### The Geography of Green Amenity

Every urban transformation produces winners and losers. The living city transition is no exception. Understanding who benefits and who bears costs is essential for both ethical assessment and strategic political calculation. The distributional impacts of green urbanism are complex, operating across multiple dimensions—property values, health outcomes, employment, neighborhood character, and quality of life.

The most immediate and visible beneficiaries of living city investments are property owners in areas receiving green infrastructure. Parks, street trees, green corridors, and ecological amenities consistently correlate with increased property values. Studies across diverse urban contexts find premiums of 5-20% for properties adjacent to quality green spaces. For homeowners, this appreciation represents windfall gains—wealth created by public investment captured by private owners who did nothing to earn it.

The property value premium creates a fundamental tension at the heart of green urbanism. The same improvements that make neighborhoods more livable also make them more expensive. Without countervailing policies, green investment systematically displaces exactly the populations it ostensibly serves. Low-income renters in neighborhoods targeted for ecological improvement face rising rents they cannot afford. The pattern repeats across cities worldwide: environmental improvement becomes a mechanism of displacement, concentrating amenities among those who already possess wealth while pushing the poor to environmentally degraded peripheries.

This dynamic—often termed "environmental gentrification" or "green gentrification"—represents one of the most serious challenges to equitable implementation of living city visions. The phenomenon is not incidental but structural. In market-based housing systems, any neighborhood improvement that increases desirability will, absent intervention, increase housing costs. The poor lose access to improving neighborhoods not despite but because of environmental investments.

### Beyond Property: Health, Employment, and Life Chances

The distributional impacts of living cities extend far beyond property markets. Health outcomes represent perhaps the most consequential dimension. Green infrastructure delivers measurable health benefits—reduced heat mortality, improved air quality, decreased cardiovascular and respiratory disease, enhanced mental health. These benefits are not distributed equally. Current patterns concentrate environmental amenities in wealthy, predominantly white neighborhoods while environmental burdens—pollution, heat islands, flooding—cluster in low-income communities of color.

A genuine living city transition could reverse these patterns, directing ecological investment toward historically neglected communities. But the same political and economic dynamics that created environmental injustice in the first place operate to preserve it. Wealthy neighborhoods possess greater political voice, more organized advocacy capacity, and better access to decision-makers. They can more effectively capture public investment, shape planning processes to their advantage, and resist unwanted land uses.

Employment represents another crucial dimension. The construction and maintenance of green infrastructure creates jobs—potentially many jobs, if the transition proceeds at the scale ecological crisis demands. These are not abstract economic categories but concrete livelihoods for specific workers and their families. The question of who gets these jobs, at what wages and under what conditions, determines whether the living city transition reinforces or challenges existing class hierarchies.

Green jobs could flow to well-paid unionized workers with benefits and career ladders, or they could reproduce the low-wage, precarious employment characteristic of contemporary service economies. The outcome depends on political choices, not technical necessities. Labor standards, prevailing wage requirements, project labor agreements, and workforce development programs can ensure that ecological transition creates good jobs. Their absence ensures that green investment, like so much urban development, generates wealth for some while offering others only contingent, inadequate employment.

### The Losers: Those Who Profit from the Status Quo

Any honest accounting of distributional impacts must acknowledge that some powerful interests stand to lose from genuine ecological transformation. The fossil fuel industry and its allies have obvious stakes in perpetuating carbon-intensive urbanism—sprawl, automobile dependence, energy-inefficient buildings. The tens of trillions of dollars in stranded assets represented by fossil fuel reserves create powerful incentives for obstruction and delay.

Less obviously, significant segments of the real estate and development industry benefit from existing arrangements even as they nominally support sustainability initiatives. Conventional development models are optimized for current regulatory frameworks, financing mechanisms, and consumer preferences. Radical transformation of urban form threatens not just specific projects but entire business models. Developers who have mastered the art of building sprawl have little interest in policy changes that would render their expertise obsolete.

Financial institutions holding mortgages on sprawl-dependent properties, automobile manufacturers and dealers, suburban retailers, highway construction firms, and the lawyers, consultants, and lobbyists who service these industries all face losses from genuine urban transformation. Their resistance is not irrational but a straightforward defense of material interests. Understanding this opposition as interest-based rather than ideological clarifies the political challenge: these actors will not be persuaded by better arguments but only defeated by superior political power.

## Building Coalitions for Urban Transformation

### The Progressive Urban Coalition

Transforming cities requires building political coalitions capable of overcoming entrenched resistance. No single constituency possesses sufficient power to achieve this alone. Success depends on assembling diverse groups whose interests, while not identical, converge sufficiently around shared goals to enable sustained collective action.

The potential elements of a progressive urban coalition include:

**Environmental advocates** bring moral urgency, technical expertise, and mobilization capacity focused on ecological outcomes. Their limitations include historically white, middle-class composition that sometimes reinforces environmental gentrification and alienates potential allies in communities of color.

**Environmental justice organizations** center the concerns of frontline communities bearing disproportionate environmental burdens. Their insistence on linking environmental and social justice challenges both conventional environmentalism and mainstream development interests.

**Labor unions** can provide organizational infrastructure, political resources, and legitimacy that environmental groups often lack. Building labor-environmental alliances requires demonstrating that ecological transition means good jobs, not job losses.

**Community development organizations** possess neighborhood-level relationships, practical expertise in housing and economic development, and credibility in low-income communities. Their participation grounds abstract visions in concrete local needs.

**Public health advocates** frame ecological urbanism in terms of health outcomes, potentially broadening appeal and connecting to powerful institutional actors in the healthcare sector.

**Faith communities** can mobilize moral authority and committed constituencies around environmental stewardship and social justice frames.

**Youth climate activists** bring energy, media savvy, and a moral claim grounded in intergenerational justice that older actors cannot easily dismiss.

**Progressive businesses** in green industries have economic interests aligned with ecological transition and can provide resources and legitimacy in business-oriented political spaces.

### Coalition Maintenance: Managing Internal Tensions

Building coalitions is easier than maintaining them. Any alliance sufficiently broad to achieve political power will contain internal tensions and contradictions. Managing these tensions—keeping diverse groups working together despite differences—is among the most difficult tasks of political leadership.

The environmental-labor divide represents a classic tension. Unions have historically viewed environmental regulation as a threat to jobs, while environmentalists have sometimes displayed indifference to working-class concerns. Overcoming this divide requires both material and symbolic work—policies that genuinely deliver good jobs through ecological transition, and political narratives that honor the dignity of labor while challenging carbon-intensive industries.

Tensions between established homeowners and renters, longtime residents and newcomers, and communities of different racial and ethnic compositions can fracture coalitions along neighborhood and identity lines. Gentrification pressures exacerbate these divisions, pitting preservation of existing community against transformation toward more ecological forms.

The environmental justice movement has developed frameworks for managing coalition tensions that merit broader application. Principles of community self-determination, leadership by frontline communities, and explicit attention to distributional impacts can ground coalitions in accountability to those with most at stake. Jemez Principles developed by Southwest Network for Environmental and Economic Justice offer a model: be inclusive, emphasize bottom-up organizing, let people speak for themselves, work together in solidarity and mutuality, build just relationships among ourselves, and commit to self-transformation.

### The Role of Anchor Institutions

Large institutional actors—universities, hospitals, cultural institutions, major employers—can play crucial roles in living city coalitions. These "anchor institutions" possess significant resources, long-term stakes in place, and reputational interests that may align with ecological urbanism. Their participation can provide legitimacy, expertise, and material support that grassroots organizations often lack.

Universities have increasingly embraced sustainability commitments, driven by student pressure, reputational competition, and genuine concern among faculty and administrators. Campus sustainability initiatives can model ecological practices, train future professionals, and provide research support for urban transformation. University investment policies, procurement practices, and real estate development can advance or obstruct living city goals depending on institutional priorities.

Healthcare systems represent another potential anchor. Hospitals and health systems are increasingly recognizing environmental determinants of health, creating institutional interests in improving air quality, reducing heat exposure, and expanding green space. Healthcare organizations also possess significant political influence, financial resources, and community standing that could be mobilized for ecological urbanism.

Engaging anchor institutions requires understanding their particular interests and constraints. Administrators face multiple, often conflicting demands from trustees, funders, employees, and constituencies. Sustainability initiatives must compete with other priorities for attention and resources. Success often depends on identifying individuals within institutions who can champion ecological goals and providing them with arguments and support to advance their positions internally.

### Strange Bedfellows: Tactical Alliances and Their Limits

Effective political movements sometimes require tactical alliances with actors whose interests or values differ significantly from one's own. Business interests can support green infrastructure for reasons of competitive advantage, property values, or reputation management rather than ecological commitment. Pragmatic politicians may embrace sustainability initiatives for electoral benefit regardless of deeper conviction. Such alliances can advance immediate objectives while creating risks of co-optation, dilution, and redirection of transformative goals toward more modest reforms.

The LEED certification system illustrates both potentials and limits of such alliances. Collaboration between environmental advocates, building industry actors, and mainstream real estate interests produced a widely adopted standard that has genuinely shifted construction practices. But critics argue that LEED's market orientation and industry-friendly standards produce "green" buildings that remain fundamentally embedded in unsustainable development patterns—high-performance suburban office parks connected to nothing, mixed-use developments that displace existing communities, trophy towers that greenwash continued carbon intensity.

The strategic question is not whether to engage in tactical alliances but how to do so without losing capacity for more transformative demands. Maintaining independent organizational bases, clearly articulating non-negotiable principles, and building power that does not depend on elite patronage are essential disciplines. Alliances should be instrumental—means to specific ends—rather than end states that foreclose more fundamental change.

## Overcoming NIMBYism: The Local Opposition Problem

### Understanding NIMBY Politics

No discussion of urban political economy can ignore the phenomenon of neighborhood opposition to development and change—the familiar NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard) dynamic that blocks housing construction, infrastructure investment, and urban transformation across metropolitan areas. NIMBYism represents one of the most significant obstacles to living city visions, as ecological urbanism requires significant changes to existing neighborhoods, land uses, and community character.

Understanding NIMBYism requires distinguishing between different motivations and manifestations. Some neighborhood opposition reflects straightforward economic interest—homeowners seeking to preserve property values by excluding uses or populations they perceive as threatening. This explicitly exclusionary NIMBYism has deep roots in American history, from racial covenants to exclusionary zoning, and continues to shape metropolitan development patterns.

Other opposition reflects concerns about genuine negative impacts—traffic, noise, pollution, strains on public services—that development can impose on existing residents. These concerns deserve serious engagement even when they conflict with broader goals. Dismissing all opposition as self-interested obstruction forecloses possibilities for accommodation and generates unnecessary conflict.

Still other neighborhood resistance reflects reasonable skepticism about development processes that have historically produced displacement, disinvestment, and broken promises. Communities that have experienced urban renewal, predatory development, or abandonment have sound reasons to distrust proposals for change, however beneficial they may appear. Building trust with such communities requires acknowledging historical harms, demonstrating genuine accountability, and delivering tangible benefits before asking for support.

### The Political Power of Localism

NIMBYism draws power from institutional structures that privilege local voice in land use decisions. American planning law generally grants substantial authority to municipal governments and, through them, to organized neighborhood interests. Public hearing requirements, environmental review processes, and political accountability of local officials to voting constituents all create opportunities for motivated opponents to block or significantly modify proposed changes.

The concentration of costs and dispersion of benefits characteristic of many urban improvements creates asymmetric incentive structures favoring opposition. A new housing development, transit line, or green infrastructure project imposes visible impacts on immediate neighbors while distributing benefits across broader populations who may not even be aware of the proposal. Opponents face strong incentives to mobilize; supporters often lack equivalent motivation.

These dynamics produce systematic distortions in urban development. Projects that would benefit metropolitan regions or society broadly are blocked by localized opposition. Housing production falls below regional needs, driving up costs and displacement. Infrastructure investment flows to paths of least political resistance rather than areas of greatest need. The cumulative effect preserves exclusionary patterns and forecloses more transformative possibilities.

### Strategies for Overcoming Local Opposition

Several strategic approaches offer potential for overcoming NIMBY dynamics while respecting legitimate community concerns:

**Preemptive state action** removes certain decisions from local control, bypassing neighborhood opposition through higher-level government mandates. California's recent housing legislation, which overrides local zoning restrictions in certain circumstances, represents this approach. The strategy generates its own opposition from local governments and residents who view state preemption as undemocratic, but may be necessary when local politics systematically underproduces needed housing or infrastructure.

**Coalition building at regional scale** can create political counterweights to neighborhood opposition by organizing beneficiaries of regional-scale change. YIMBY (Yes In My Back Yard) movements attempt this strategy, mobilizing renters, prospective residents, and others who benefit from increased housing production against incumbent homeowner interests. Success depends on actually organizing dispersed beneficiaries—no easy task given the concentration of opponents and dispersion of supporters.

**Community benefits agreements** negotiate explicit commitments from developers to address neighborhood concerns in exchange for community support. Well-designed CBAs can deliver genuine benefits—local hiring, affordable housing, environmental improvements—while overcoming opposition that might otherwise block beneficial projects. Poorly designed CBAs can become opportunities for narrow interests to extract concessions without generating broad community benefit.

**Procedural reforms** can reduce opportunities for obstruction without eliminating meaningful community voice. Streamlining environmental review for projects that advance environmental goals, limiting standing to sue, and creating ministerial rather than discretionary approval processes for certain project types can reduce the transaction costs that enable obstruction.

**Changing the calculus of local politics** may ultimately require making support for housing and urban transformation politically rewarding for local officials. This means organizing constituencies who reward pro-housing votes, supporting candidates who embrace growth, and making NIMBY positions politically costly rather than politically safe.

## Developer Resistance and Real Estate Politics

### The Development Industry's Stakes

The real estate development industry occupies a peculiar position in living city politics. Developers possess resources, expertise, and political access essential for actually building transformed urban environments. Yet their business models and professional identities are often organized around practices fundamentally at odds with ecological urbanism. Understanding and navigating this tension is essential for anyone seeking to transform urban form.

Conventional development in most North American contexts is optimized for sprawl. Suburban residential subdivisions, auto-oriented commercial strips, and single-use office parks represent known products with established financing mechanisms, proven market demand, and familiar regulatory pathways. Developers have accumulated expertise, relationships, and capital specifically adapted to these forms. Radical changes in urban form threaten not just individual projects but accumulated investments in know-how and market position.

The development industry also benefits from existing regulatory frameworks even when criticizing them. Zoning codes, subdivision regulations, and building codes create barriers to entry that protect established firms from competition. Large developers with legal and engineering staff can navigate complex regulatory environments that present insurmountable obstacles to smaller actors. Calls for deregulation, while sometimes genuine, often aim at removing specific impediments while preserving barriers that protect market position.

### Aligning Development Interests with Ecological Goals

The development industry is not monolithic. Significant segments have economic interests potentially aligned with ecological urbanism. Urban infill developers have business models based on compact development that zoning reform could enable. Green building specialists profit from sustainability requirements that create demand for their expertise. Affordable housing developers often need density allowances that living city frameworks could provide.

Policy design can strengthen these aligned interests while weakening opponents. Zoning reforms that enable density while maintaining design standards favor developers with urban infill expertise over greenfield sprawl builders. Green building mandates create markets for sustainable construction while increasing costs for conventional practice. Inclusionary requirements direct profits toward affordable housing production rather than luxury speculation.

Financial incentives can shift developer calculations. Tax increment financing, density bonuses, reduced fees for sustainable projects, and preferential access to public land can make ecological development more profitable than conventional alternatives. These incentives represent public expenditure that merits scrutiny—subsidizing private profit is not obviously preferable to direct public provision—but can align private interest with public goals when designed carefully.

### Confronting Implacable Opposition

Some development interests will oppose ecological transformation regardless of incentives or accommodation. Greenfield developers whose business models depend on continued sprawl, landlords profiting from scarcity, speculators betting on continued automobile dominance—these actors face losses from living city transitions that no policy design can offset. Their opposition must be defeated rather than accommodated.

Defeating entrenched interests requires building countervailing power. Campaign finance reform, conflict of interest rules, and transparency requirements can reduce developers' political advantages. Organizing tenants, workers, and environmentally-affected communities can create constituencies whose votes outweigh developer money. Direct action—protests, boycotts, blockades—can impose costs on obstruction that economic incentives alone cannot.

The development industry's political power ultimately rests on its capacity to withhold investment. Threats of capital strike—refusing to build if regulations become too onerous—represent the ultimate weapon against policies that threaten developer interests. Countering this threat may require public alternatives—direct government construction, nonprofit development, community land trusts—that can produce needed housing and infrastructure regardless of private sector participation.

## Labor Union Partnerships: Building Worker Power

### The Green Jobs Promise

Labor unions represent potentially powerful allies for ecological urbanism. Union membership has declined dramatically in recent decades, but unions retain significant organizational capacity, political resources, and moral authority. Building labor-environmental alliances around the living city transition could strengthen both movements while addressing historical tensions.

The foundation for such alliances lies in the job creation potential of ecological transformation. Constructing green infrastructure, retrofitting buildings for energy efficiency, building transit systems, and maintaining urban ecological systems are all labor-intensive activities. The International Labour Organization estimates that limiting global warming to 2°C would create 24 million jobs globally while eliminating 6 million, for a net gain of 18 million positions. Many of these jobs would be in construction, manufacturing, and services where unions have traditionally organized.

For unions, green jobs represent not just employment but potential growth. Organizing new industries and workers offers a path out of the decline that has characterized American labor for decades. Unions that position themselves as partners in ecological transition can claim roles in new sectors while those that oppose or ignore the shift risk irrelevance.

### What Makes a "Good" Green Job?

Not all green jobs are good jobs. The mere fact of environmental benefit does not ensure adequate wages, benefits, job security, or working conditions. Solar installation, for example, is often performed by non-union workers at wages below construction industry norms, with limited benefits and few career advancement opportunities. Wind energy employment is somewhat better but hardly uniformly excellent. Urban greening work often relies on precarious contract labor or even unpaid volunteers.

Creating good green jobs requires deliberate policy choices. Project labor agreements can require union wages and apprenticeship programs on public projects. Community workforce agreements can mandate local hiring and targeted employment of disadvantaged populations. Prevailing wage laws extend union standards to non-union contractors. Workforce development programs can create pathways into green careers for workers displaced from carbon-intensive industries.

These policies face opposition from employers seeking to minimize labor costs and occasionally from environmentalists focused narrowly on environmental outcomes without attention to labor standards. Building labor-environmental coalitions requires environmental advocates to genuinely embrace worker concerns, not merely instrumentalize labor support for environmental goals. Conversely, unions must recognize that ecological crisis threatens workers and communities at least as severely as any other population, and that obstruction of environmental protection is ultimately self-defeating.

### Case Study: The BlueGreen Alliance

The BlueGreen Alliance, founded in 2006 by the Sierra Club and United Steelworkers, represents the most prominent institutional expression of labor-environmental coalition building in the United States. The alliance has expanded to include major unions representing workers in construction, communications, transportation, and public employment alongside leading environmental organizations.

The BlueGreen Alliance has advocated for policies combining environmental protection with job creation—clean energy standards, manufacturing support, infrastructure investment, fair trade provisions. Its existence demonstrates that labor-environmental cooperation is possible despite historical tensions. Whether such cooperation can be maintained through the more difficult choices that genuine ecological transition will require remains uncertain.

Critics from both labor and environmental perspectives question whether the BlueGreen Alliance represents genuine convergence or merely a lowest-common-denominator platform that avoids hard questions. The alliance has not, for example, taken clear positions on expanding fossil fuel extraction or specific pipeline projects that divide labor and environmental constituencies. Maintaining coalition unity may come at the cost of transformative ambition.

### Beyond Alliance: Worker-Led Transition

More ambitious visions imagine workers not merely as partners in elite-designed transitions but as leaders of transformation. The concept of a "just transition"—originally developed by labor leader Tony Mazzocchi—envisions workers in carbon-intensive industries taking leadership roles in planning and implementing the shift to sustainable alternatives. Rather than victims of change to be compensated and accommodated, workers become agents of transformation.

Worker-led transition requires resources and institutional support. Transition assistance programs providing income, retraining, and relocation support for displaced workers are necessary but not sufficient. Workers also need power—through unions, works councils, or other forms of representation—to shape how transitions unfold in their workplaces and communities. The Green New Deal proposals that emerged in 2018-2019 incorporated many of these elements, though political prospects for their adoption remain uncertain.

The living city transition offers particular opportunities for worker power. Green construction and infrastructure maintenance are inherently local and difficult to offshore. Care of urban ecosystems requires ongoing labor that cannot be automated away. These characteristics create favorable conditions for worker organization and bargaining power, if supportive policies are in place.

## Progressive Policy Pathways: Tools for Transformation

### Planning and Zoning Reform

The legal infrastructure of urban planning represents both an obstacle to and opportunity for living city transitions. Existing zoning codes often mandate the separation of uses, low densities, and automobile accommodation that characterize unsustainable urbanism. Reforming these codes is essential for enabling ecological transformation.

Form-based codes replace use-based zoning with regulations focused on building form, scale, and relationship to public space. By specifying physical parameters while allowing flexibility in use, form-based codes can enable the mixed-use, compact development characteristic of sustainable urbanism. Their adoption has expanded significantly, though conventional zoning remains dominant.

Upzoning—increasing allowed density in residential areas—can enable housing production and reduce sprawl pressure. Minneapolis's elimination of single-family-only zoning in 2018 represented a landmark reform, followed by similar changes in Oregon and California. These policies remain controversial, with critics arguing they enable gentrification without ensuring affordability and supporters contending that increased supply, whatever its limitations, is necessary for addressing housing crises.

Green area ratios and ecological performance standards can require new development to incorporate vegetation, permeable surfaces, and ecological features. Berlin's Biotope Area Factor, Seattle's Green Factor, and similar programs establish quantitative requirements for ecological elements in development projects. These tools integrate ecological considerations into routine development review rather than treating them as exceptional add-ons.

### Fiscal Policy and Public Investment

Tax policy shapes urban development in ways often invisible to non-specialists. Property taxes based on land value rather than improvements (land value taxation) can discourage holding land idle while encouraging development of underutilized parcels. Tax increment financing can capture value created by public investments to fund additional improvements. Carbon taxes and congestion pricing can internalize environmental costs that market prices ignore.

Public investment priorities determine what gets built and where. Infrastructure spending on highways enables sprawl while transit investment supports compact development. Park acquisition and green infrastructure construction directly create ecological amenities. Energy efficiency programs for public buildings demonstrate possibility while reducing emissions. The allocation of public resources across these competing uses reflects political priorities and shapes the physical environment in which residents live.

Climate budgets—establishing carbon reduction targets and tracking progress through government accounting systems—can institutionalize environmental priorities in fiscal governance. Oslo's climate budget, adopted in 2017, integrates emissions tracking with financial planning, making carbon consequences visible in budgetary decisions. Expanding such approaches could make ecological costs legible in the routine operations of government.

### Public Ownership and Community Control

Beyond regulating private activity, progressive policy can expand public and community ownership of urban land and infrastructure. Public land banks can acquire vacant and abandoned properties for ecological development. Municipal utilities can provide energy, water, and other services oriented toward sustainability rather than profit. Community land trusts can hold land in permanent affordability, preventing green gentrification while enabling ecological improvement.

These alternatives to private ownership address fundamental limitations of regulatory approaches. Regulations constrain but do not direct private actors; they can prevent worst practices but cannot mandate best ones. Public and community ownership enables positive vision—directly creating the ecological infrastructure that markets fail to provide and regulation cannot compel.

Vienna's social housing system demonstrates possibilities at scale. Roughly 60% of Viennese residents live in subsidized housing, much of it directly owned by the city or limited-profit housing associations. This massive public sector has enabled high-quality, ecologically sophisticated development that market housing rarely achieves. Buildings are designed for longevity, energy efficiency, and community amenity rather than maximum short-term return.

Expanding public and community ownership faces significant obstacles in North American contexts. Ideological opposition to government activity, fiscal constraints on public investment, and legal frameworks privileging private property all impede such approaches. Yet models exist and are expanding—from community land trusts in Burlington, Vermont to public banks in North Dakota to municipal broadband systems across the country. Building on these examples offers pathways beyond the limitations of purely regulatory approaches.

### Regulatory Innovation

Building codes, development standards, and environmental regulations establish the parameters within which private actors operate. Strengthening these regulations can mandate ecological practices that voluntary approaches fail to achieve. Energy codes requiring high-performance buildings, stormwater regulations mandating green infrastructure, and tree protection ordinances limiting removal of urban vegetation all represent regulatory tools for ecological urbanism.

Regulatory approaches face criticism from multiple directions. Property rights advocates contest government restrictions on private land use. Affordability advocates worry that environmental requirements increase housing costs. Developers complain of complexity and uncertainty. These criticisms merit consideration but do not negate the fundamental point that markets left to themselves will not produce ecological outcomes without regulatory guidance.

Performance-based regulation offers potential for combining environmental stringency with implementation flexibility. Rather than mandating specific practices, performance standards establish outcomes—energy consumption limits, stormwater infiltration rates, tree canopy coverage—and allow regulated parties to choose how to achieve them. This approach can encourage innovation while ensuring results, though it requires monitoring and enforcement capacity that prescriptive rules may not.

## Incremental Versus Transformative Change

### The Case for Incrementalism

The scale and urgency of ecological crisis might seem to demand rapid, transformative change. Yet most actual urban transformation proceeds incrementally—small changes accumulating over time rather than sweeping ruptures with existing arrangements. Understanding both the logic and limits of incrementalism is essential for strategic thinking about living city transitions.

Incrementalism has significant advantages. It operates within existing institutional frameworks, avoiding the political costs of fundamental confrontation. It allows learning and adjustment as effects of initial changes become apparent. It builds constituencies for further change as early adopters demonstrate benefits. It reduces risks by testing approaches at small scale before broader application.

Successful urban transformations have often followed incremental pathways. The spread of bike-sharing systems, the adoption of LEED certification, the expansion of farmers markets, and the proliferation of parklets and public space activations all represent incremental changes that, cumulatively, have shifted urban practice. None individually represents transformation, but together they have altered expectations about what cities can be.

The political economy of incrementalism favors changes that do not directly threaten powerful interests. Bike lanes on streets with excess capacity, greening programs in neglected spaces, efficiency improvements that reduce costs—such changes can proceed with minimal opposition because they impose few losses on anyone who matters politically. They represent "low-hanging fruit" achievable without fundamental conflict.

### The Limits of Incrementalism

Yet incrementalism has severe limitations when confronting systemic challenges. Climate change, biodiversity collapse, and environmental injustice are not problems amenable to gradual amelioration. They represent system-level dysfunctions requiring system-level responses. Addressing them through incremental changes is, as scholars have noted, like rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic—activity that may be locally beneficial but fails to address the fundamental problem.

Incrementalism can also generate its own obstacles to further change. Early, easy reforms may defuse pressure for more fundamental transformation by creating impressions of adequate progress. Constituencies mobilized around incremental demands may demobilize once initial victories are achieved. Reforms become institutionalized in ways that create new vested interests opposed to further change.

The phenomenon of "lock-in" illustrates how incremental choices foreclose future possibilities. Infrastructure investments create path dependencies that shape development for decades. Once highway systems, sprawl patterns, and automobile-oriented land uses become established, reversing them becomes enormously difficult and expensive. Incremental improvements within locked-in systems may marginally reduce harms while perpetuating fundamental problems.

### Toward Transformative Incrementalism

The dichotomy between incrementalism and transformation may be false. Strategic actors can pursue incremental changes that build toward transformative outcomes rather than substituting for them. The key lies in choosing which increments to pursue and how to link them into sequences that accumulate toward fundamental change.

"Transformative incrementalism" involves several elements:

**Strategic sequencing** identifies which early changes create conditions for subsequent advances. Building coalitions through initial victories, developing institutional capacity through pilot programs, and shifting public expectations through demonstration projects all represent incremental steps that enable further change.

**Institutional development** creates organizations, procedures, and capacities that persist beyond initial reforms. Community land trusts, participatory budgeting processes, green infrastructure maintenance programs—these institutional innovations can outlast their founders and provide bases for continued advance.

**Narrative construction** frames incremental changes as steps toward larger visions rather than ends in themselves. The story matters: are we solving problems or transforming systems? Is this program an alternative to further change or a demonstration of what more could achieve?

**Crisis exploitation** recognizes that windows for transformative change open unpredictably when existing arrangements fail visibly. Incremental preparation—developing plans, building coalitions, training cadres—positions movements to act decisively when opportunities arise. As Milton Friedman observed, "Only a crisis—actual or perceived—produces real change. When that crisis occurs, the actions that are taken depend on the ideas that are lying around."

### The Temporality of Transformation

Living city transitions must navigate a fundamental tension between urgency and institutional constraint. Ecological crisis demands rapid action; political and economic systems resist rapid change. This temporal mismatch structures the strategic landscape.

One response accepts institutional constraint as given and focuses on maximizing change possible within existing parameters. This approach prioritizes achievable reforms over aspirational goals, working through established channels rather than confronting power directly. Its risk is that achievable reforms prove insufficient to the challenge.

An alternative response prioritizes building power sufficient to overcome institutional constraints, accepting slower initial progress in exchange for greater eventual transformation. This approach invests in movement building, political organization, and consciousness change rather than immediate policy wins. Its risk is that deferred action allows continued damage while waiting for conditions that may never arrive.

Most effective movements combine both approaches—pursuing immediate reforms while building longer-term capacity, winning concrete improvements while maintaining transformative vision. The balance between these emphases shifts with circumstance. When institutional constraints are rigid and transformative potential low, incremental reforms may represent the best available option. When crisis delegitimizes existing arrangements and opens space for fundamental change, movements must be prepared to act boldly.

## Conclusion: Politics as Ecology, Ecology as Politics

The political economy of living cities resists simple prescriptions. There is no formula that guarantees success, no coalition that automatically prevails, no policy package that achieves transformation without opposition. The struggle for ecological urbanism is genuinely political—contested, uncertain, dependent on human choices and power relations rather than technical necessity or historical inevitability.

Yet this indeterminacy is also opportunity. If outcomes were predetermined, political action would be pointless. Precisely because the future remains open, what we do matters. Building coalitions, confronting opposition, designing policies, and choosing strategies all shape what cities become. The political work is not a distraction from the real work of ecological design but an essential component of it.

The analysis in this chapter suggests several orienting principles for those engaged in this struggle:

**Center justice.** Distributional questions are not afterthoughts but central to both ethical legitimacy and political viability. Living city visions that ignore who wins and loses will fail to build necessary coalitions and will reproduce the exclusions that have characterized earlier environmental movements.

**Build broad coalitions while maintaining transformative vision.** Winning requires allies, but alliances that sacrifice fundamental goals represent defeat disguised as victory. The challenge is combining breadth with depth—bringing diverse groups together around genuinely transformative programs rather than lowest-common-denominator platforms.

**Take opposition seriously.** Entrenched interests will not be persuaded by better arguments or more compelling visions. They must be defeated through superior political power. Understanding who opposes change and why is essential for developing strategies to overcome them.

**Embrace the long game while acting urgently.** Ecological crisis demands immediate action; institutional transformation requires sustained effort over years and decades. Both temporalities must be held together—pursuing immediate reforms while building capacity for fundamental change.

**Learn from failure.** No strategy succeeds consistently. Movements that cannot learn from defeats and adapt to changing circumstances will not prevail. Honest assessment of what works and what doesn't, difficult though it may be, is essential for improvement.

The political economy of living cities is, finally, about power—who has it, how it operates, and how it can be shifted. Transforming cities requires transforming the power relations that shape them. This is difficult, uncertain work. But it is also necessary work if the living city vision is to become reality rather than remaining merely an appealing but unrealized aspiration.

The stakes could not be higher. Cities are where most humans now live and where the future of human civilization will largely be determined. Making them livable—ecologically sustainable, socially just, democratically governed—is among the most important tasks facing humanity in the coming decades. The political economy of this transition is not a specialized subdomain but the terrain on which success or failure will be decided.

## Key Concepts

- **Environmental gentrification**: The displacement of low-income residents from neighborhoods where environmental improvements increase property values and housing costs
- **NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard)**: Local opposition to development or change in one's immediate neighborhood
- **Project labor agreement**: Pre-hire collective bargaining agreement establishing terms for a specific construction project
- **Community benefits agreement**: Legally binding contract between developers and community organizations specifying benefits the community will receive from a development
- **Just transition**: Framework for ensuring that workers and communities dependent on carbon-intensive industries are supported through the shift to sustainable alternatives
- **Land value taxation**: Property tax based on land value alone rather than improvements, intended to discourage speculation and encourage development
- **Transformative incrementalism**: Strategic approach combining incremental reforms with longer-term vision for fundamental change
- **Lock-in**: Path-dependent processes through which early choices constrain future possibilities

## Discussion Questions

1. How might community benefits agreements be designed to prevent green gentrification while still enabling ecological improvements in low-income neighborhoods?

2. What conditions are necessary for stable labor-environmental coalitions, and what factors tend to fracture such alliances?

3. Is NIMBYism ever legitimate? How should planning processes balance local concerns against broader regional or societal interests?

4. Can incremental reforms accumulate into transformative change, or do they more often substitute for fundamental transformation?

5. How might the political economy of living cities differ in contexts outside North America—in the Global South, in authoritarian regimes, in cities with different property regimes?
