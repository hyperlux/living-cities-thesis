# Global South & Decolonial Pathways

## How Living Cities Refuse Extraction and Honor the Wisdom That Was Always There

---

> *"The smart city is a Trojan horse. Inside the gleaming technology is the oldest colonial logic: we know what you need better than you do."*
>
> — Urbanist proverb, Lagos, 2024

## The New Colonialism Wears a Digital Face

When Sidewalk Labs arrived in Toronto's Quayside neighborhood in 2017, they brought renderings of gleaming towers, promises of "innovation," and a 1,500-page proposal that mentioned governance exactly zero times in its first draft.

When Songdo rose from reclaimed land in South Korea, it promised to be the world's first fully wired city—and delivered a ghost town where sensors outnumber residents and the streets feel like hospital corridors.

When NEOM's promotional videos showcased The Line—a 170-kilometer mirror-walled city in the Saudi desert—they neglected to mention the Howeitat tribe being forcibly relocated, or that construction workers were dying in conditions human rights organizations called "medieval."

These are not anomalies. They are the pattern.

**The smart city has become the new colonial project.** Not through gunboats and flags, but through consultants and platforms. Not through extraction of rubber and gold, but through extraction of data and sovereignty. The technology is new. The logic is ancient: external actors defining what progress means for people who were never consulted.

The Global South has seen this before. From Berlin Conference borders drawn with rulers on maps of places the rulers had never visited. From structural adjustment programs that prescribed identical "reforms" for economies as different as Senegal and Indonesia. From development projects that measured success by inputs (dollars spent, roads built) rather than outcomes (lives improved, agency expanded).

The playbook is consistent:
1. Arrive with capital and expertise
2. Define the problem according to your frameworks
3. Implement solutions that benefit your interests
4. Extract value while claiming to provide it
5. Leave when the extraction slows

Living cities refuse this playbook entirely. Not by rejecting technology, but by insisting that technology serve local wisdom rather than replace it. Not by refusing external connection, but by demanding that connection flow both ways.

This chapter maps pathways for cities in the Global South to become living—pathways that honor what was always known while embracing what's newly possible. Jakarta, Lagos, São Paulo: not as "developing" cities waiting to become like London or Singapore, but as already-sophisticated urban organisms with governance innovations that the Global North has yet to discover.

The assumption of this chapter is radical in its simplicity: **The Global South doesn't need to be taught how to build living cities. It needs to stop being prevented from building what it already knows how to build.**

## The Autopsy of Smart City Colonialism

### Sidewalk Toronto: The Governance Void

In October 2017, Waterfront Toronto announced that Sidewalk Labs—a subsidiary of Alphabet, Google's parent company—would develop Quayside, a 12-acre parcel on Toronto's waterfront. The project was framed as a partnership.

It was not a partnership. It was an attempted acquisition of urban sovereignty.

**What Sidewalk offered:**
- $50 million in planning funds
- Cutting-edge technology for mobility, energy, and services
- Access to Google's engineering talent
- Promise of "the most innovative district in the world"

**What Sidewalk wanted:**
- Data collected from sensors throughout the district
- Right to monetize resident behavior patterns
- Control over governance frameworks before they existed
- Precedent for similar projects globally

The original proposal was 1,524 pages long. It described technology in exhaustive detail—sensors, autonomous vehicles, adaptive buildings, underground freight systems. It mentioned "data governance" as something to be figured out later.

**The red flags were immediate:**

Jim Balsillie, co-founder of BlackBerry, called it "a colonizing experiment in surveillance capitalism."

Bianca Wylie, an open data advocate, noted: "Sidewalk Labs is approaching this as if data governance is a technical problem. It's not. It's a political problem about who holds power."

Former Ontario privacy commissioner Ann Cavoukian resigned from the project's advisory panel, stating: "I imagined us creating a smart city of privacy, as opposed to a smart city of surveillance."

**The collapse:**

By May 2020, Sidewalk walked away—ostensibly due to COVID-related economic uncertainty, actually due to sustained citizen opposition that made the project politically unviable.

**What the autopsy reveals:**

Sidewalk Toronto failed because it attempted to insert a governance void into a city with functioning democratic institutions. The technology was ready. The surveillance was ready. The extraction mechanism was ready. What wasn't ready was a population that would accept platform sovereignty over urban sovereignty.

Toronto had something many cities in the Global South are told they lack: functioning institutions capable of saying no. This is the colonial assumption made explicit—that "developing" cities are so desperate for investment that they'll accept any terms.

The lesson for living cities: **Governance cannot be deferred.** Any project that promises technology now and governance later is a colonial project in digital clothing.

### Songdo: The City That Forgot People

Songdo International Business District, built on 1,500 acres of reclaimed land along South Korea's Yellow Sea coast, was designed to be the world's smartest city. Since construction began in 2003, over $35 billion has been invested.

**The technology works:**

- 40% green space (genuine achievement)
- Pneumatic waste disposal eliminating garbage trucks
- Building systems centrally controlled for efficiency
- Ubiquitous Wi-Fi and sensor networks
- Integration with Incheon Airport

**The city doesn't:**

Songdo was designed to house 300,000 people. Twenty years later, population hovers around 100,000—many of whom are there because of discounted housing, not because they chose the place.

**The problem wasn't technology. It was theory of change.**

Songdo was built on the assumption that if you provide optimal physical infrastructure, people will come. But cities are not machines that need optimal inputs. They are organisms that need the conditions for life to emerge.

Songdo lacks:
- Economic reason to exist (no indigenous industry, mostly displaced from Seoul)
- Organic street life (everything is planned; nothing emerges)
- Local identity (international architecture with no Korean soul)
- Governance participation (residents arrived to find decisions already made)

**The urbanist Jane Jacobs wrote:**

> "A city cannot be a work of art... Art is selective, cities are inclusive. Cities contain too much variety and detail for any single mind to fully comprehend."

Songdo tried to be a work of art—a single vision realized in concrete and glass. It achieved formal perfection and lost the messiness that makes cities alive.

**The colonial logic embedded:**

Songdo was built by developers for an imagined "international business class"—global executives who would appreciate its English signage, international schools, and sanitized streets. It was, in essence, a city built for people who don't exist—or who exist but prefer places with actual life.

The assumption: if we build it clean enough, efficient enough, modern enough, "they" will come. This is the same assumption that undergirded colonial hill stations in India, garden cities in Africa, and planned capitals throughout the decolonizing world. The assumption that experts know better than the people who will actually live there.

**The lesson for living cities:**

You cannot build a city. You can only build scaffolding within which a city might grow. Songdo built beautiful scaffolding and forgot to create conditions for growth.

Living cities start with people—their existing patterns, their emergent wisdom, their unarticulated knowledge about what makes a place feel alive. Technology serves this knowledge rather than replacing it.

### NEOM: The Autocratic Hallucination

NEOM is Saudi Arabia's $500 billion urban megaproject, planned for a 26,500 km² zone in the country's northwest. Its centerpiece, The Line, is a 170-kilometer linear city designed to house 9 million people in two mirror-coated towers stretching across the desert.

**The vision is cinematically compelling:**

- No cars, no streets, no carbon emissions
- AI-coordinated everything
- 450-meter height, 200-meter width, zero surface parking
- High-speed rail connecting everything
- "The world's most livable city" (per Saudi promotional materials)

**The reality is something else:**

**Displacement:** The Howeitat tribe, indigenous to the region, has been forcibly relocated. Abdul Rahim al-Huwaiti, a tribal member who publicly opposed displacement, was killed by Saudi security forces in 2020. Other tribe members have been sentenced to death for refusing to leave.

**Labor conditions:** Investigative reporting by The Guardian, Business Insider, and others has documented working conditions approaching forced labor—passport confiscation, inadequate housing, limited freedom of movement, and deaths from heat and overwork.

**Feasibility:** Engineering experts have questioned whether a 170-kilometer enclosed structure is buildable, livable, or sane. Where does fresh air come from? How do 9 million people evacuate in emergency? What happens to mental health in a corridor with no horizon?

**The colonial logic made explicit:**

NEOM is not merely extractive—it is the extraction logic taken to its conclusion. A city built without consulting its future residents (who are imagined as interchangeable global consumers). A city built by demolishing indigenous presence. A city that treats labor as a resource to be expended rather than people to be served.

The promotional materials use the language of innovation and sustainability. The reality is autocratic hallucination—the belief that with enough money and force, you can will a city into existence without any of the organic processes that make cities actually work.

**Why NEOM is relevant to living cities:**

NEOM represents the end state of smart city logic: if you can monitor everything, predict everything, and control everything, you don't need democracy. You don't need resident input. You don't need organic emergence. You just need enough computing power and enough police.

This is what living cities refuse. Not the technology—much of NEOM's proposed technology would serve residents beautifully if residents owned and governed it. What living cities refuse is the assumption that urban life can be designed from above and imposed without consent.

## The Jakarta Pathway: Kampung Wisdom for the Flood-Adapted City

Jakarta is sinking. Literally.

The Indonesian capital sits on swampy ground, much of it below sea level. North Jakarta has subsided by 4 meters in some areas over the past three decades. By 2050, large portions of the city may be underwater.

Simultaneously, Jakarta is a megacity of 11 million people (34 million in the metropolitan area), characterized by patterns that urban planners trained in Western frameworks might call "chaotic" but that are, upon closer inspection, sophisticated adaptations to specific conditions.

**The kampung as urban organism:**

A kampung is an informal urban village—a dense, self-organized neighborhood characterized by narrow pathways, mixed use, and social proximity. To Western urban planners, kampungs look like slums to be cleared. To residents, they are highly adapted habitats that provide what formal cities often cannot.

**What kampungs provide:**
- **Economic integration:** Work and residence intermingled. The warung (small shop) on the corner, the motorcycle repair operation in the alley, the food vendor using family space during day hours. This isn't failure to separate zones—it's success at integrating them.

- **Flood adaptation:** Kampungs flood regularly. Residents have developed sophisticated responses: raised sleeping platforms, waterproof storage, evacuation networks, rebuilding practices. This isn't lack of flood control—it's living with floods rather than against them.

- **Social insurance:** In the absence of formal welfare systems, kampung networks provide mutual aid. When someone loses income, neighbors provide food. When someone dies, the community covers burial costs. This isn't lack of social security—it's social security at a different scale.

- **Governance:** Kampungs have RT (Rukun Tetangga, neighborhood unit) and RW (Rukun Warga, community unit) structures—hyperlocal governance bodies that resolve disputes, coordinate maintenance, and represent collective interests to higher authorities. This isn't lack of governance—it's governance too fine-grained for formal systems to perceive.

**The formal city's failure:**

Jakarta's formal city—the towers, the malls, the gated communities—represents everything kampungs are not. Separated zones, formal employment, atomized residence, dependence on infrastructure that fails when it floods.

The formal city has been sinking precisely because its development pattern ignored what kampung wisdom understood: you cannot conquer the water here. You must live with it.

Heavy groundwater extraction (because formal buildings need formal water systems) caused the subsidence. Paving over permeable surfaces (because formal streets need formal surfaces) caused the flooding. Clearing kampungs for towers (because formal development needs empty land) destroyed the social infrastructure that helped people cope.

**The living city pathway for Jakarta:**

A living Jakarta wouldn't replace kampungs with towers. It would learn from kampungs while upgrading their capacity:

**1. Recognize kampung governance as legitimate**
- RT/RW structures as official data cooperative nodes
- Kampung assemblies as participatory budgeting units
- Kampung knowledge as licensed expertise (flood adaptation, social insurance patterns)

**2. Integrate informal economy rather than formalize it**
- Mobile payment systems that work with warung cash flows
- Cooperative structures that aggregate small operators without destroying their flexibility
- Data cooperatives where informal vendors own their transaction data

**3. Design for flood adaptation, not flood prevention**
- Amphibious architecture that rises with water
- Infrastructure that fails gracefully rather than catastrophically
- Kampung rebuilding wisdom integrated into building codes

**4. Connect without extracting**
- Platform cooperatives for ride-sharing (Gojek model, but owned by drivers)
- Energy cooperatives for solar deployment on kampung rooftops
- Data cooperatives for aggregate anonymized kampung data that benefits researchers and residents alike

**The Jakarta lesson for living cities:**

The "informal" sector is not awaiting formalization. It is already organized—by different principles, at different scales, for different purposes than Western urban theory recognizes.

Living cities don't integrate the informal by making it formal. They expand formal recognition to include what was always there.

## The Lagos Pathway: Energy Leapfrogging and Market Queen Networks

Lagos is Africa's largest city—over 15 million people in the official count, likely 20+ million in reality. It is growing at 3%+ annually, adding a city the size of Pittsburgh every year. By 2100, it may be the world's largest city.

Lagos is also characterized by what formal planning considers "dysfunction"—chronic power outages, legendary traffic, sprawling informal settlements, and governance that appears chaotic to outside observers.

Look closer. Lagos works. Not despite its informality but through it.

**The energy situation:**

Nigeria has approximately 5,000 MW of grid capacity for 200 million people. For comparison, New York City alone uses about 11,000 MW at peak demand.

Most Lagosians therefore don't depend on the grid. They depend on generators—small ones for homes, larger ones for businesses, and an ecosystem of fuel suppliers, mechanics, and technicians that keeps the whole system running.

This is typically framed as failure. But consider: Lagos has effectively distributed its power system. There is no single point of failure. When the grid goes down (frequently), life continues. The resilience is built in.

**The leapfrogging opportunity:**

Lagos can bypass the centralized grid model entirely—not because it failed to build one, but because it never depended on one anyway.

**The path:**
- Distributed solar + battery systems replacing generators
- Microgrids connecting neighborhoods without central utility dependence
- Peer-to-peer energy trading where excess solar production sells to neighbors
- All of this owned cooperatively—by the same informal networks that currently run generators

This isn't catching up to the Global North's grid infrastructure. It's leaping over it to infrastructure the Global North hasn't yet built.

**The market queen networks:**

Lagos's markets are legendary—massive, chaotic, incredibly efficient. What outside observers miss is the governance structure that makes them work.

Market queens (Iyaloja) are women leaders who govern market operations. They:
- Allocate stall space
- Mediate disputes
- Set quality standards
- Coordinate with police and politicians
- Represent collective interests

This is governance—as sophisticated as any city council, operating at a scale (individual markets may have tens of thousands of traders) that formal systems couldn't match.

**The formalization trap:**

International development agencies and Nigerian reformers periodically attempt to "formalize" Lagos markets—register traders, assign permanent stalls, enforce standardized rules.

These efforts typically fail because they misunderstand what they're dealing with. Market queen governance is flexible, adaptive, and responsive to conditions that change weekly. Formalization makes it rigid, slow, and disconnected from actual needs.

**The living city pathway:**

**1. Recognize market queen governance as legitimate**
- Market associations as official cooperative structures
- Iyaloja councils as recognized governance bodies with formal standing
- Market knowledge as licensed expertise (supply chain management, conflict resolution)

**2. Build on existing networks rather than replacing them**
- Mobile payment systems that work with existing credit relationships
- Data cooperatives where market traders own aggregate data
- Insurance products designed for market timing and risk patterns

**3. Energy cooperatives for markets**
- Shared solar + battery systems replacing individual generators
- Cooperative ownership with governance integrated into market queen structures
- Technical training for women and youth already in informal repair economies

**4. Create cooperative platform infrastructure**
- Ride-sharing cooperatives (okada/keke drivers as owners)
- Logistics cooperatives (last-mile delivery networks formalized without destroying flexibility)
- Payment cooperatives (mobile money owned by users, not telecom platforms)

**The Lagos lesson for living cities:**

The "chaos" of Lagos is a highly adapted system. Its informality is not absence of structure—it's presence of structure at scales and types that formal systems don't recognize.

Living cities make visible what was already working. They provide legal recognition, technical infrastructure, and cooperative structures that amplify existing organization rather than replacing it with imported models.

## The São Paulo Pathway: Favela-City Symbiosis and Participatory Legacy

São Paulo is a city of 12 million—22 million in the metropolitan area—characterized by stark inequality. Gleaming towers of Paulista Avenue overlook favelas clinging to hillsides. Gated communities share water tables with informal settlements that lack plumbing.

But São Paulo is also a city with deep participatory traditions. The Workers' Party (PT), which governed Brazil from 2003-2016 and again from 2023, was founded in São Paulo. Porto Alegre's participatory budgeting—now implemented in over 1,500 cities globally—grew from Brazilian social movements. The 1988 Constitution was drafted with unprecedented civic participation.

**The water crisis as teacher:**

In 2014-2015, São Paulo experienced its worst drought in 80 years. The Cantareira reservoir system, serving 8 million people, dropped to 5% capacity. Water rationing, officially denied but actually implemented through pressure reduction, left millions without reliable supply.

**Who suffered most:** Favela residents, already on the margin of formal water systems, without storage capacity, without political voice to demand solutions.

**Who adapted fastest:** Also favela residents, using informal networks to share water, find alternative sources, and pressure authorities through organized action.

The crisis revealed the favela-city relationship: formal systems extract from favelas (labor, domestic service, informal commerce) while providing inadequate infrastructure in return. When formal systems fail, favelas absorb the failure disproportionately—but also demonstrate adaptive capacity that formal planning lacks.

**Favela as urban organism:**

Favelas are not "slums awaiting improvement." They are sophisticated urban formations that provide:

**1. Housing for the urban economy**
Domestic workers, construction laborers, service workers, delivery drivers—the formal city depends on labor that it doesn't pay enough to afford formal housing. Favelas solve this problem, providing proximity to employment without impossible housing costs.

**2. Entrepreneurial infrastructure**
Favelas contain dense networks of small businesses: corner stores, beauty salons, bars, repair shops, food vendors. This isn't informal because it's disorganized—it's informal because formal regulation (zoning, licensing, taxation) would destroy it.

**3. Cultural production**
Funk, samba, graffiti, fashion—favelas generate culture that the formal city then consumes. This isn't side effect—it's economic output that Brazil exports globally.

**4. Social organization**
Community associations (Associações de Moradores) govern favela life: managing relations with police, negotiating with utilities, organizing mutual aid, representing collective interests. This is governance, operating in conditions where formal government has limited presence.

**The participatory budgeting legacy:**

Porto Alegre's participatory budgeting (Orçamento Participativo, or OP), launched in 1989, allowed residents to directly decide how to allocate portions of the municipal budget. By 2000, it had become globally famous—the World Bank and IMF praised it, cities from Kerala to Cologne copied it.

**What OP demonstrated:**
- Ordinary citizens can make complex budgeting decisions
- Participation increases when it leads to real power
- Spending shifts toward priorities of previously excluded groups
- Corruption decreases when budgets are publicly deliberated

**What OP's challenges revealed:**
- Participation is exhausting—sustaining engagement requires resources
- Technical complexity can be weaponized against participants
- Party capture is possible (OP served PT, but also became PT's)
- Scale matters—too small and it's performative; too large and it's unmanageable

**The living city pathway for São Paulo:**

**1. Recognize favela governance**
- Community associations as official cooperative nodes
- Favela-based data cooperatives owning resident data
- Favela knowledge (construction techniques, social organization, cultural production) as recognized expertise

**2. Infrastructure cooperatives**
- Water cooperatives that don't depend on formal utility failure
- Energy cooperatives deploying solar on favela rooftops
- Internet cooperatives providing connectivity without surveillance

**3. Participatory budgeting 2.0**
- Digital platforms (Decidim-style) that reduce participation costs
- Outcome-based budgeting (not just input allocation, but measured results)
- Federated structure connecting neighborhood PB to city PB to state PB

**4. Favela-city economic recognition**
- Formal value measurement of favela contributions (labor, culture, housing)
- Cooperative structures for domestic workers, informal vendors, cultural producers
- Revenue sharing from cultural products (funk profits flowing back to origins)

**5. Water as commons**
- Watershed-level governance transcending city boundaries
- Water data cooperatives tracking quality and availability
- Rainwater harvesting cooperatives appropriate to favela scale

**The São Paulo lesson for living cities:**

The favela is not a problem to be solved but a partner in symbiosis. The formal city extracts from favelas while providing inadequate return. Living cities restructure this relationship—recognizing favela contributions, sharing infrastructure fairly, and extending cooperative ownership to those currently excluded.

The participatory legacy shows that another governance is possible. The challenge is sustaining it against capture, scaling it beyond individual cities, and connecting it to genuine power over outcomes.

## Indigenous Governance: The Wisdom That Was Always There

Before there were city councils, there were indigenous governance systems that managed land, resolved conflicts, and coordinated collective action across vast territories.

These systems were not primitive precursors to modern governance awaiting replacement. They were (and are) sophisticated technologies for living well together—technologies that modern governance systems would do well to learn from rather than supplant.

**The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy:**

The Haudenosaunee Confederacy—uniting six nations (Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and Tuscarora) across what is now northeastern North America—predates European contact by centuries. Its governance structure influenced the design of the United States Constitution (acknowledged in H. Con. Res. 331, 1988).

**Key principles:**
- **Seventh generation thinking:** Decisions evaluated by impact seven generations hence (approximately 150 years). This is not metaphor—it's procedural requirement.
- **Consensus-based decision making:** Not majority rule but process-until-agreement. Time-intensive but produces durable decisions.
- **Separation and balance of powers:** Clan mothers select leaders; leaders execute decisions; councils deliberate. Mutual accountability across functions.
- **Women's central role:** Clan mothers (not incidentally female) hold ultimate authority over leader selection and can remove leaders who fail to serve.

**The Andean ayllu:**

The ayllu is the basic social unit of Andean indigenous communities—a network of extended families managing common territory through collective labor and reciprocal exchange.

**Key principles:**
- **Ayni** (reciprocity): Labor and goods exchanged within the ayllu on principles of mutual obligation, not transaction. What you receive, you will give in turn.
- **Minka** (collective labor): Work done for common benefit—building infrastructure, managing irrigation, cultivating common lands. Participation is obligation but also belonging.
- **Rotating leadership:** Authority circulates; everyone who can lead, eventually does. This prevents concentration while building collective capacity.

**Aboriginal Australian land management:**

For at least 65,000 years, Aboriginal Australians managed a continent through governance systems that European colonizers failed to recognize as governance.

**Key principles:**
- **Fire management:** Controlled burns creating mosaic landscapes—reducing catastrophic wildfire risk while promoting biodiversity. This isn't "natural" landscape—it's carefully cultivated.
- **Songlines:** Geographic knowledge encoded in story, song, and ceremony. Navigation, resource location, seasonal timing—encoded in culture rather than written documentation.
- **Collective custodianship:** Land is not owned but cared for. Responsibility flows in both directions—land provides, custodians protect. This is relationship, not property.

**What indigenous governance teaches living cities:**

**1. Time horizons matter**
Modern governance struggles with long-term thinking. Electoral cycles encourage short-term optimization. Indigenous seventh-generation thinking (Haudenosaunee) shows that long time horizons can be proceduralized—built into governance structure rather than depending on virtuous individuals.

**Living city application:** Constitutional provisions requiring 50+ year impact assessment. Sunset clauses forcing re-ratification across generations. Future persons represented in present deliberation.

**2. Consensus is possible**
The assumption that consensus can't scale comes from not having seen it scale. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy governed hundreds of thousands of people across vast territory through consensus processes.

**Living city application:** Consensus-seeking as default, with fallback to supermajority only after deliberation completes. Time investment front-loaded for decision durability.

**3. Leadership can rotate**
The assumption that hierarchy is necessary comes from systems designed for concentration. Andean ayllu rotating leadership shows that authority can circulate, building capacity while preventing capture.

**Living city application:** Rotating governance roles across neighborhoods, demographics, and interest groups. Everyone who can lead, eventually does.

**4. Reciprocity is system design**
Indigenous economics are frequently characterized as "gift economies," but this misses the point. Ayni and similar systems are not charity—they are insurance, investment, and social infrastructure. You give because you will receive; you receive because you will give.

**Living city application:** Contribution systems designed for reciprocity rather than transaction. Time banking, skill sharing, and mutual aid as formal infrastructure.

**5. Relationship precedes property**
Aboriginal Australian custodianship inverts the property assumption. Land is not thing to be owned but relationship to be maintained. This isn't primitive—it's more sophisticated than fee simple ownership.

**Living city application:** Data trusteeship rather than ownership. Infrastructure custodianship with bidirectional obligations. The city as entity-in-relationship rather than asset to be optimized.

**The decolonial commitment:**

These lessons cannot be extracted from indigenous communities the way rubber was extracted from Congo. Learning from indigenous governance requires:
- **Acknowledgment:** These systems exist, they work, they were suppressed rather than superseded
- **Compensation:** For lands stolen, peoples displaced, knowledge appropriated
- **Sovereignty:** Indigenous communities governing themselves by their own systems, not incorporated into settler governance
- **Permission:** Learning with consent rather than studying without it

Living cities in the Global South can learn from indigenous governance because many Global South cities exist on indigenous land, contain indigenous people, and suppress indigenous governance that could be restored rather than invented.

## Leapfrogging Without Extraction

The development discourse has long promised "leapfrogging"—the Global South skipping past obsolete technologies to adopt cutting-edge ones. Mobile phones without landlines. Solar without coal plants. Digital payments without bank branches.

The promise is real. The danger is extraction wearing leapfrogging's clothing.

**The extraction pattern in technology adoption:**

**1. External capital funds deployment**
Outside investors provide infrastructure—cell towers, solar farms, payment platforms. The capital is framed as "investment" or "aid."

**2. Technology is imported, expertise is not**
Hardware and software come from outside. Technical capacity to maintain, modify, and evolve systems does not. The dependency is structural.

**3. Data flows outward**
User data collected locally is processed and stored externally. Value is extracted; insights return only if profitable for extractors.

**4. Profits expatriate**
Returns on investment flow to external shareholders. Local economy provides labor and consumption, not ownership.

**5. Standards are set externally**
Technical protocols, governance frameworks, and interoperability requirements are determined by those who arrived first with capital.

This is leapfrogging as colonialism. The technology is new; the extraction is ancient.

**Genuine leapfrogging requires:**

**1. Local ownership from the start**
- Cooperative structures that ensure residents own infrastructure they depend on
- Joint ventures where local partners have genuine equity, not just contracts
- Domestic capital mobilization (diaspora investment, local savings, municipal bonds)

**2. Technology transfer, not technology rental**
- Training embedded in every deployment
- Open source systems that can be modified locally
- Documentation in local languages
- Explicit licensing for local production

**3. Data sovereignty**
- Data stored locally, governed locally
- Processing capacity built domestically
- Insights shared internationally only on negotiated terms
- Value extraction impossible because data never leaves local control

**4. Standard-setting participation**
- Global South voices in protocol development
- Regional standards that don't require Global North approval
- Interoperability without dependency

**5. Manufacturing capacity**
- Not just deployment but production
- Solar panels manufactured in Africa, not just installed there
- Batteries produced in South America, not just purchased from Asia
- The means of production, not just the means of consumption

**Case: M-Pesa's mixed legacy**

M-Pesa, Kenya's mobile money system, is frequently cited as leapfrogging success. And it is—genuinely. Kenya achieved near-universal financial inclusion through mobile phones, skipping past bank branch infrastructure entirely.

But M-Pesa is owned by Safaricom, majority-owned by Vodafone (UK). Profits flow to London. User data is corporate asset. Terms of service are set externally.

**The alternative that didn't happen:**

Imagine M-Pesa as a cooperative—owned by its users, governed by Kenyan institutions, with profits reinvested domestically. Same technology. Same leapfrogging. Different extraction pattern.

This wasn't impossible. It was a choice—made when capital was offered with strings attached, accepted because alternatives seemed unavailable.

**Case: Bangladesh solar revolution**

Bangladesh has deployed over 6 million solar home systems—one of the largest off-grid solar deployments globally. This is genuine leapfrogging, providing electricity to areas the grid won't reach for decades.

**The ownership question:**

Most systems were financed through NGO-administered loans—Grameen Shakti, IDCOL. The systems are owned by households (good), but the financing and technical capacity are externally controlled (concerning).

**What genuine leapfrogging would add:**
- Local manufacturing of solar components (not just assembly)
- Cooperative ownership of installation and maintenance capacity
- Data from millions of systems governed by Bangladeshi data cooperatives
- Technical training producing Bangladeshi solar engineers, not just Bangladeshi installers

**The living city leapfrogging principle:**

**Technology without dependency requires owning the means of coordination—not just consuming its outputs.**

This means:
- Cooperative ownership of infrastructure
- Open source technology that can be modified without permission
- Data sovereignty that keeps value local
- Training that builds capacity rather than creating dependency
- Manufacturing that captures production value, not just deployment value

## Financing Without Debt Traps

China's Belt and Road Initiative has financed infrastructure across the Global South—ports, railways, power plants, telecommunications. It has also created debt relationships that critics call neo-colonial.

Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port, unable to generate revenue to service Chinese loans, was leased to China for 99 years. Zambia's electricity utility, heavily indebted to Chinese lenders, has been reported (though denied) as collateral for debt restructuring. The pattern—infrastructure funded by debt that becomes leverage—is consistent enough to warrant concern.

But the problem isn't Chinese financing specifically. It's debt-based infrastructure financing generally—whether from Chinese policy banks, Western multilateral institutions, or private capital markets.

**The debt trap pattern:**

1. **Infrastructure requires capital.** Cities need roads, power plants, water systems. This capital must come from somewhere.

2. **Debt is offered on attractive terms.** Interest rates below market, grace periods before repayment, long maturities. The deal looks good.

3. **Revenue projections are optimistic.** The infrastructure is supposed to generate returns—port fees, electricity sales, toll revenue. Projections assume everything works.

4. **Returns disappoint.** Projects underperform. Revenue falls short. Debt service consumes budget intended for other purposes.

5. **Restructuring concentrates leverage.** Unable to repay, borrowers accept terms that transfer control—ownership stakes, resource concessions, policy conditions.

This pattern isn't inevitable. It's designed.

**Alternative financing mechanisms:**

**1. Land value capture**

Infrastructure increases land values. Roads make adjacent land more accessible. Transit stations make nearby land more desirable. Water systems make development possible.

Currently, these value increases are captured by private landowners who did nothing to create them. Land value capture redirects this value to pay for the infrastructure that created it.

**Mechanisms:**
- Land value taxation (capturing ongoing appreciation)
- Tax increment financing (capturing future appreciation for present investment)
- Community land trusts (capturing appreciation collectively)
- Value capture agreements (negotiated sharing with developers)

**Why it works for living cities:**
No external debt. Value stays local. Those who benefit pay proportionally. Speculation is discouraged because appreciation is taxed.

**2. Diaspora investment**

Global South cities have diaspora populations with capital, connections, and emotional investment in home communities. This capital is currently underutilized for urban development.

**Mechanisms:**
- Diaspora bonds (Ethiopia, India, Israel have successful examples)
- Community investment cooperatives (pooling small investments)
- Remittance-linked development (portion of transfers directed to infrastructure)
- Diaspora ownership stakes in urban cooperatives

**Why it works for living cities:**
Patient capital with aligned interests. Diaspora investors want their home communities to thrive, not just to extract returns. Cultural connection creates accountability.

**3. Municipal cooperative bonds**

Cities can issue bonds purchased by residents—essentially borrowing from the people who will benefit from infrastructure.

**Mechanisms:**
- Low-denomination bonds accessible to ordinary residents
- Interest rates competitive with bank savings
- Transparency about use and performance
- Cooperative ownership of funded infrastructure

**Why it works for living cities:**
Residents become creditors with stake in success. Returns flow to community rather than external investors. Accountability is built in because bondholders are also voters.

**4. Sovereign wealth models**

The Alaska Permanent Fund captures resource extraction value in a fund that pays dividends to residents. The Norway Government Pension Fund does similar for oil revenue. 

**Urban application:**
- Urban commons funds capturing land value, data value, spectrum value
- Dividends to residents creating stake in city success
- Intergenerational savings rather than debt accumulation

**5. South-South development finance**

The New Development Bank (BRICS), Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, and various regional development banks offer alternatives to Bretton Woods institutions.

**What would make them genuinely different:**
- Conditionality aligned with borrower priorities, not lender ideology
- Local currency lending avoiding exchange rate risk
- Technical assistance building capacity rather than dependency
- Ownership structures giving borrowers genuine voice

**The living city financing principle:**

**Capital should come with accountability to the city, not leverage over it.**

This means:
- Ownership distributed to residents
- Returns flowing locally
- Decision rights staying local
- Debt avoiding where possible, structured carefully where necessary
- External capital welcomed on terms that serve local interests

## South-South Knowledge Networks

The default knowledge flow in development is North to South—"best practices" developed in rich countries, applied (often unsuccessfully) to conditions utterly unlike their origin.

But the most relevant knowledge for Lagos is probably in Jakarta. The most relevant knowledge for São Paulo is probably in Nairobi. Cities facing similar challenges—rapid growth, informal economies, climate adaptation, resource constraints—have more to learn from each other than from cities that faced different challenges generations ago.

**What North-South knowledge transfer misses:**

- **Context:** Solutions developed for stable governments, abundant capital, and formal economies fail in conditions of state fragmentation, capital scarcity, and informal majorities.

- **Power dynamics:** "Capacity building" often means teaching Global South professionals to think like Global North consultants—adopting frameworks that obscure rather than illuminate local conditions.

- **Extractive relationships:** Northern universities study Southern cities, publish in Northern journals, build Northern careers. Knowledge flows out; careers stay put.

- **Temporal mismatch:** The Global North solved (or failed to solve) urbanization challenges under conditions that no longer exist. Historical experience from 1850-1950 is less relevant than contemporary experience from 2000-2025.

**What South-South knowledge networks offer:**

**1. Relevant context**
Cities facing similar conditions learn from each other's experiments. What works in rapid urbanization? What works with informal majorities? What works under resource constraint?

**2. Equitable power dynamics**
Peer learning rather than expert teaching. Knowledge exchanged rather than transferred. No assumption that one party knows and the other learns.

**3. Keeping value local**
Research conducted by local institutions, published in accessible venues, building local careers. Knowledge stays where it's generated.

**4. Temporal relevance**
Contemporary experiments rather than historical case studies. What's working now in Lagos matters more than what worked in London in 1890.

**Existing South-South networks:**

**Shack/Slum Dwellers International (SDI):**
A network of community-based organizations in 32 countries across Africa, Asia, and Latin America. SDI members—federations of informal settlement residents—share knowledge about savings schemes, land acquisition, housing construction, and political organizing.

**Key innovations:**
- Horizontal learning exchanges (delegations visiting peer communities)
- Federated savings schemes (managed by communities, not NGOs)
- Community-enumeration (residents counting themselves, owning data)
- Precedent-setting projects (small-scale demonstrations becoming citywide practice)

**C40 Cities:**
A network of 96 megacities committed to climate action. While including Global North cities, C40 enables South-South exchange among mayors facing similar challenges.

**Key functions:**
- Peer exchange programs
- Technical assistance from peer cities (not just consultants)
- Collective procurement (driving down technology costs)
- Knowledge commons (shared research and case studies)

**UCLG (United Cities and Local Governments):**
The global network of local governments, headquartered in Barcelona. UCLG facilitates municipal cooperation, particularly among Global South cities.

**Africities:**
The largest gathering of African local governments, convening every three years. Exchange of experience among mayors, technical staff, and civil society.

**What living cities can build on:**

**1. City-to-city data cooperatives**
Multiple cities pooling anonymized data for shared learning. What works for traffic in Bogotá might work in Manila. Aggregated data reveals patterns no single city could see.

**2. Open source governance platforms**
Decidim (Barcelona's participatory platform) is already open source and deployed globally. Living cities can develop shared infrastructure—data cooperative platforms, targeting authority systems, anti-capture mechanisms—as global commons.

**3. South-South technical exchange**
Engineers from Lagos learning solar installation in Bangladesh. Urban planners from Jakarta studying participatory budgeting in Brazil. Knowledge flowing horizontally rather than vertically.

**4. Joint research institutions**
African Urban Lab. South American Urban Futures. Asian Informal Economy Institute. Research capacity built in the Global South, studying Global South cities, publishing accessibly.

**5. Coalition financing**
Multiple cities jointly issuing bonds. Collective bargaining with technology providers. Shared infrastructure reducing per-city costs.

**The knowledge network principle:**

**The most relevant expertise for Global South cities exists in other Global South cities. Living cities build horizontal knowledge flows that treat every city as both teacher and learner.**

## Universal Principles, Local Expression

Living cities share universal principles. But universal principles are not universal implementations.

The error of smart city colonialism is confusing the two—assuming that because certain principles (sustainability, efficiency, participation) are universal, the implementations (particular technologies, governance structures, ownership mechanisms) must also be universal.

Living cities know better. Principles travel. Implementations must be grown locally.

**The universal principles:**

**1. Circulation, not extraction**
Value flows rather than accumulates. Energy moves through the system rather than pooling. What one produces, another consumes; what dies feeds new life.

*This principle is universal. The mechanisms of circulation are local.*

**2. Ownership, not dependence**
Residents own the systems they depend on. Income flows from stake, not wage. Agency is protected by ownership structure.

*This principle is universal. The ownership forms are local—cooperatives in one context, community trusts in another, municipal enterprises in a third.*

**3. Governance, not management**
Residents have voice in decisions that affect them. Participation is real, not performative. Power is distributed and checked.

*This principle is universal. The governance institutions are local—assemblies here, councils there, rotating leadership elsewhere.*

**4. Purpose beyond efficiency**
The city serves human flourishing, not optimization metrics. Quiet kindness, generous living, conditions for meaning—these are the aims.

*This principle is universal. What flourishing looks like is local—shaped by culture, history, environment, and aspiration.*

**5. Technology serving wisdom**
Technology amplifies existing knowledge rather than replacing it. Local expertise is recognized; imported expertise supplements without supplanting.

*This principle is universal. The technologies chosen and how they're adapted are local.*

**What local expression means in practice:**

**For Jakarta:**
Kampung governance recognized and amplified. Flood adaptation wisdom integrated into infrastructure. Informal economy supported, not formalized. Technology serving existing patterns rather than imposing new ones.

**For Lagos:**
Market queen networks formalized as governance. Energy cooperatives built on generator-repair ecosystems. Leapfrogging to distributed solar without external debt. Mobile platforms owned by users, not foreign shareholders.

**For São Paulo:**
Favela-city symbiosis made explicit and fair. Participatory budgeting extended and deepened. Water governed as commons. Cultural production value shared with producers.

**For all cities:**
Indigenous wisdom recognized as expertise. Colonial governance structures questioned and replaced. External financing accepted only on terms that serve local interests. Knowledge flowing horizontally among peers, not vertically from experts.

**The synthesis:**

Living cities are neither universal template nor collection of isolated experiments. They are universal principles finding local expression—the same underlying biology generating different species adapted to different niches.

A Jakarta kampung and a Copenhagen superblock both express circulation, ownership, governance, purpose, and technology-serving-wisdom. They express these differently because they grow from different soils.

The challenge is neither imposing universal implementation nor abandoning shared principles. It is holding both—universal enough to learn from each other, local enough to actually work.

## The Cathedral in the Kampung

Return to the image from Part I of this thesis: the cathedral of quiet kindness.

In Global North cities, the cathedral metaphor evokes stone structures—Notre-Dame, Westminster, Cologne. Grand buildings taking centuries to complete, expressing aspiration that transcends any individual lifetime.

In the Global South, the cathedral is everywhere and nowhere. It is the kampung that rebuilds after floods because neighbors know what neighbors need. It is the market queen who settles disputes so efficiently that formal courts are irrelevant. It is the favela association that provides insurance when formal systems fail.

**The cathedral was always being built. The colonial smart city would have demolished it to make room for something "better."**

Living cities recognize that the cathedral is already there—incomplete, stressed, imperfect, but structurally sound. The work is not to build from scratch but to strengthen what exists, to connect what's isolated, to extend what's local.

**The Global South advantage:**

In some ways, Global South cities are better positioned for living city transition than Global North cities.

- **Less lock-in:** Infrastructure is still being built; path dependencies can be avoided.
- **Living informal systems:** Governance structures that actually work exist outside formal recognition.
- **Fresh governance:** Less scar tissue from failed experiments.
- **Demographic energy:** Young populations capable of building and adapting.
- **Climate pressure:** Urgency that concentrates attention and resources.

**The Global South challenge:**

But the challenges are real.

- **Capital scarcity:** Living cities still require investment; patient capital is hard to find.
- **External pressure:** International institutions, foreign investors, and neo-colonial actors push extractive models.
- **Elite capture:** Local elites may prefer smart city extraction to living city distribution.
- **State fragility:** Governance structures may lack capacity to support transition.
- **Compounding crises:** Climate, disease, conflict—multiple emergencies demanding immediate response.

**The decolonial pathway:**

The pathway is neither rejection of technology nor acceptance of extraction. It is technology on terms that serve residents, adopted through governance that represents them, financed by mechanisms that don't create dependency, and connected through networks that honor local wisdom while sharing universal principles.

This pathway requires:

**1. Refusal**
Saying no to Sidewalk Labs, to debt traps, to technology-without-governance, to solutions defined externally.

**2. Recognition**
Making visible what already works—kampung governance, market queen networks, favela associations, indigenous wisdom. Formal recognition of informal excellence.

**3. Connection**
Building horizontal networks among Global South cities. Sharing knowledge, pooling resources, developing standards together.

**4. Building**
Creating cooperative infrastructure—data cooperatives, energy cooperatives, housing cooperatives, platform cooperatives—owned by those who depend on them.

**5. Patience**
Living cities take decades to mature. The pathway is not a project with completion date but a direction with horizon.

---

## Conclusion: The Living City Was Always Here

The Global South does not need to be taught how to build living cities.

Jakarta's kampungs are already living urban tissue—self-organizing, adaptive, resilient. They need recognition, not replacement.

Lagos's market queens are already governing at scale—efficiently, responsively, legitimately. They need formalization that amplifies rather than constrains.

São Paulo's favelas are already in symbiosis with the formal city—providing labor, culture, flexibility. They need fair exchange, not extraction.

Indigenous governance systems were always sophisticated—managing territories, resolving conflicts, thinking seven generations ahead. They need sovereignty, not study.

The living city was always here. It was suppressed, ignored, dismissed, studied, extracted from—but never absent.

**What living cities require in the Global South is not construction but liberation.** The removal of colonial governance structures that prevent indigenous wisdom from expressing itself. The refusal of extractive financing that creates dependency. The rejection of smart city models that assume expertise flows in one direction.

And then: the building of infrastructure that amplifies what exists. Data cooperatives that recognize residents' ownership of their own patterns. Energy cooperatives that leapfrog without extraction. Governance platforms that formalize participation already happening informally. Financing mechanisms that keep value local.

**The cathedral is already under construction.** It has been for centuries, millennia—as long as humans have lived together in cities. The work is not to design it but to stop demolishing it. Not to build it but to let it grow.

The scaffolding—cooperatives, platforms, protocols—is what we can contribute. The cathedral—quiet kindness, generous living, conditions for meaning—is what humans build when the conditions allow.

Those conditions have always existed in the Global South, despite everything. Living cities make them visible, protect them from extraction, and connect them to each other.

This is the decolonial pathway: not catching up to the Global North, but liberating what was always here to become what it was always capable of becoming.

**The future of cities is not in Sidewalk or Songdo or NEOM. It is in the kampung, the market, the favela, the ayllu, the commons that humans have always known how to build.**

The living city was always here. It's time to let it breathe.

## Appendix: The Living City Diagnostic for Global South Contexts

A city in the Global South considering the living city pathway can assess its position across several dimensions:

### Dimension 1: Informal Economy Recognition

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Informal economy criminalized, actively suppressed |
| 1 | Informal economy tolerated but unrecognized |
| 2 | Informal economy formally recognized, still excluded from governance |
| 3 | Informal economy representatives in formal governance structures |
| 4 | Informal and formal economies integrated, with cooperative ownership |
| 5 | No distinction between informal/formal; all economic activity has voice |

### Dimension 2: Data Sovereignty

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | All significant data held by foreign platforms |
| 1 | Some local data storage, no governance framework |
| 2 | Data protection law exists, limited enforcement |
| 3 | Functioning data governance, still individual rather than collective |
| 4 | Data cooperatives operating, limited coverage |
| 5 | Universal data cooperative membership, full sovereignty |

### Dimension 3: Indigenous/Traditional Governance Integration

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Traditional governance suppressed or destroyed |
| 1 | Traditional governance exists, no formal recognition |
| 2 | Traditional governance recognized in limited domains |
| 3 | Traditional governance has formal standing, limited power |
| 4 | Traditional and formal governance integrated, mutual authority |
| 5 | Traditional governance principles embedded in constitutional framework |

### Dimension 4: Financing Independence

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Infrastructure entirely dependent on external debt |
| 1 | Mixed financing with heavy external debt burden |
| 2 | Land value capture operating, still debt-dependent |
| 3 | Diaspora and domestic capital mobilized, debt declining |
| 4 | Predominantly local financing, external debt manageable |
| 5 | Full financing independence with sovereign wealth accumulation |

### Dimension 5: South-South Connection

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Knowledge flows entirely North-South |
| 1 | Awareness of peer cities, no active exchange |
| 2 | Participation in regional networks, limited depth |
| 3 | Active bilateral exchanges with peer cities |
| 4 | Contributing to and learning from multiple South-South networks |
| 5 | Integrated into federated South-South knowledge commons |

### Using the Diagnostic

Cities rarely score uniformly across dimensions. A city might have strong informal economy integration (4) but weak data sovereignty (1). The diagnostic reveals where intervention is most needed and most possible.

**Priority sequencing:**

For most Global South cities, the recommended sequence is:
1. **Informal economy recognition** (foundation for everything else)
2. **Traditional governance integration** (brings legitimacy and wisdom)
3. **South-South connection** (provides models and support)
4. **Financing independence** (enables autonomy)
5. **Data sovereignty** (requires infrastructure that other steps build)

This is not universal—context determines sequence. But the general principle holds: begin with what's already there (informal economy, traditional governance), connect to peers (South-South networks), then build infrastructure (financing, data).

## Glossary of Terms

**Ayllu:** Andean indigenous social unit; extended family network managing common territory through collective labor and reciprocal exchange.

**Ayni:** Reciprocity principle in Andean cultures; labor and goods exchanged through mutual obligation rather than transaction.

**Kampung:** Indonesian term for urban village; informal neighborhood characterized by density, self-organization, and social proximity.

**Iyaloja/Market Queen:** Female leader in West African markets; governs market operations including space allocation, dispute resolution, and collective representation.

**Minka:** Collective labor in Andean cultures; work performed for community benefit as both obligation and belonging.

**RT (Rukun Tetangga):** Indonesian neighborhood unit; hyperlocal governance body managing 30-50 households.

**RW (Rukun Warga):** Indonesian community unit; federation of RT units managing larger neighborhood concerns.

**Warung:** Indonesian small shop or food stall; typically home-based, serving immediate neighborhood needs.

**Seventh Generation Thinking:** Haudenosaunee governance principle requiring decisions to be evaluated by their impact seven generations (~150 years) into the future.

**Songlines:** Aboriginal Australian navigation system; geographic and cultural knowledge encoded in story, song, and ceremony.

**Land Value Capture:** Financing mechanism redirecting land value increases (created by public investment) to public benefit rather than private windfall.

**Data Cooperative:** Member-owned organization providing data storage, collective bargaining with data users, and value distribution to member-producers.

This chapter was written in consultation with the thesis framework and blueprint, but also draws on:

- Field reports from urbanists working in Jakarta, Lagos, and São Paulo
- Published case studies of participatory governance and cooperative ownership
- Oral histories of indigenous governance as transmitted through secondary sources

Living cities in the Global South will be built by people in the Global South. This chapter is offered as one contribution to a conversation that is not ours to lead.
